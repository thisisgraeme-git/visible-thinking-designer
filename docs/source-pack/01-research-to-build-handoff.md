## 1. Core proposition

### Working name

**Visible Thinking Designer**

### One-line pitch

**An AI-assisted design partner that helps educators redesign one learning activity so consequential learner thinking becomes visible, supportable and usable as evidence of capability.**

### Short product description

An educator uploads or describes a learning activity, identifies the intended capability and chooses how AI sits in the learner journey. Through a guided conversation, the Designer identifies what important thinking may remain invisible and helps create three to five purposeful visible-thinking moments across attempt, inquiry, checking, judgement, application and feedback. It returns an editable Visible Thinking Plan—not a compliance checklist or generic lesson plan.

### Intended change

Shift educator attention:

**from judging capability mainly through finished products**

**to deliberately designing selected evidence across the learning journey.**

---

## 2. The problem worth solving

AI can improve visible performance without guaranteeing retained learning, evaluative judgement or transfer. When different combinations of learner and machine effort can produce similar finished outputs, the output cannot carry the whole evidential burden.

Educators already make thinking visible through conversation, observation, checking, demonstration, feedback and changed conditions. Much of this evidence is incidental, inconsistently designed or disconnected from formal judgements about learning.

The product opportunity is therefore not more surveillance or more documentation. It is a better design conversation:

> What thinking must remain with the learner, where should it become visible, and what should happen next?

---

## 3. Research-informed foundation

The MVP should treat the Five Conditions as a **design language**, not a prescribed sequence, checklist, teaching method or validated assessment instrument.

### Five Conditions for Visible Thinking

1. **Attempt — calibrated to readiness**  
   Preserve productive learner effort without turning unsupported struggle into a virtue.

2. **Question — supported by guidance**  
   Help learners identify uncertainty, generate alternatives and ask questions that deepen inquiry.

3. **Check — grounded in the discipline**  
   Test claims, actions or outputs against evidence, standards, constraints and the behaviour of the real task environment.

4. **Explain Judgement — tested through adaptive follow-up**  
   Ask learners to connect, defend and revise decisions in response to questions that were not fully predictable in advance.

5. **Apply — under changed conditions**  
   Look for adaptation when the client, material, constraint, standard, evidence, time pressure or consequence changes.

### Foundation

All five conditions rest on:

- context;
- learner readiness;
- appropriate scaffolding;
- educator professional judgement.

### Enabling loop

**Feedback must be interpreted and used.** The meaningful evidence is not simply that feedback was delivered, but what the learner corrected, revised, questioned, retained, rejected or tried next.

### Evidence boundary

Confidence grows when evidence converges across a process. No single artefact, explanation, conversation, transcript or observed performance proves capability or authorship by itself.

## 4. Product interaction

The first form should be an **interaction**, not merely an artefact.

Its primary output is an editable **Visible Thinking Plan** for one activity.

### Entry paths

The educator can:

- upload an existing task, activity or assessment;
- paste task instructions;
- describe the activity conversationally;
- begin from an intended capability and design forward.

### Required context

The interaction should establish only what is needed:

- intended learner capability;
- activity or task;
- learner readiness and relevant supports;
- discipline, setting and stakes;
- where capability is currently difficult to see;
- the chosen AI stance.

### AI stance

AI in the learner journey must be configurable:

- encouraged;
- constrained;
- peripheral;
- absent;
- undecided.

The Designer must work across all five positions. It must not begin by assuming AI use or making AI the centre of the educational problem.

### Guided design sequence

1. **Name the capability.** What should the learner understand, judge, perform, adapt or take responsibility for?
2. **Diagnose invisibility.** What meaningful cognitive or professional work could disappear behind the finished output?
3. **Choose the moments.** Select three to five consequential visible-thinking moments. Not every activity needs every condition.
4. **Design each moment.** Specify the learner action, educator prompt, assistance boundary and useful evidence.
5. **Complete the loop.** Identify the feedback source, the expected learner response and what the next attempt or changed condition will reveal.
6. **Review proportionality.** Check workload, learner readiness, equity, stakes and risk of surveillance.
7. **Return an editable plan.** Allow the educator to revise, remove, reorder or add moments before export.

---

## 5. MVP output: Visible Thinking Plan

The plan should contain:

- activity and intended capability;
- learner/context notes required for design;
- selected AI stance;
- the capability that may currently be invisible;
- three to five visible-thinking moments;
- the Five Condition associated with each moment, where useful;
- what the learner does;
- what the educator asks, notices or introduces;
- what counts as useful evidence;
- what assistance is available and what consequential work remains with the learner;
- the feedback source;
- what the learner must do with the feedback;
- a changed-condition or transfer opportunity where proportionate;
- workload, equity or surveillance cautions;
- an explicit educator review/edit gate.

Export can initially be a clean printable page, Markdown, PDF or copyable structured text. Export polish must not delay the functioning interaction.

---

## 6. MVP definition of done

The hackathon MVP is done when an educator can:

1. upload or describe one real learning activity;
2. state the intended capability and learner context;
3. select or discuss the role of AI;
4. receive a diagnosis of what consequential thinking may be hidden;
5. co-design three to five purposeful visible-thinking moments;
6. connect feedback to a visible learner response;
7. include application under changed conditions where relevant;
8. edit the plan before accepting it;
9. export or copy a coherent Visible Thinking Plan;
10. complete the interaction without entering identifiable learner records.

### Quality floor

The output must be specific to the supplied activity. It must not collapse into generic advice such as “ask learners to explain their thinking”.

### Validation signal

Hackathon completion is not the only success measure. A meaningful early commercial signal is **one real prospective customer willing to pay a named price** for the interaction, artefact or facilitated implementation—not merely downloads or expressions of interest.

---

## 7. What the MVP is not

Do not turn the first build into:

- an AI detector;
- an authorship-verification engine;
- a prompt-history or learner-surveillance system;
- a generic lesson-plan generator;
- a rubric generator with Five Conditions pasted onto it;
- a student chatbot;
- an LMS;
- a whole-programme assessment platform;
- an automatic credential decision-maker;
- a repository of identifiable learner records;
- a system that forces one position on AI use;
- a claim that the Five Conditions have been validated.

Do not redesign an entire programme in the MVP. Redesign **one learning activity** well.

## 8. Demo scenario

Use one task with visible real-world constraints. A strong candidate from recent field work is:

> Prepare a flat white during a busy café service.

The Designer can help the educator surface:

- an initial plan or attempt before substantial assistance;
- questions about customer requirements, workflow or uncertainty;
- checks against recipe, extraction, milk texture, timing and workplace standards;
- explanation of a decision when trade-offs arise;
- application when the order, equipment, customer need or time pressure changes;
- feedback from the product, customer, equipment or tutor;
- the learner’s response and next attempt.

This scenario demonstrates that visible thinking is not confined to written assessment and that feedback can come from the work itself.

An application or demo may use another activity if it better matches the hackathon audience, but it should retain comparable constraints and evidential richness.

## 9. Application-ready framing

Map these components into the application form once its exact fields are available.

### Project

Visible Thinking Designer

### User

Educators, tutors and assessment designers who need to strengthen learning and capability evidence as AI makes polished answers easier to produce.

### Problem

Finished work is becoming a weaker standalone indicator of what learners understand, can judge and can do independently or under changed conditions.

### Solution

A guided AI interaction that turns one existing learning activity into an editable plan for selected visible-thinking and feedback moments.

### Why now

Generative AI is widening the gap between visible performance and developed capability. Institutions need practical learning-design responses that do not depend on universal prohibition, detection or surveillance.

### Differentiation

- begins with capability and learning design rather than tool policing;
- works whether AI is encouraged, constrained or absent;
- makes selected consequential moments visible rather than recording everything;
- treats feedback used—not feedback delivered—as part of the evidence loop;
- keeps professional judgement and educator editing in control;
- is grounded in recent field practice and a verified research synthesis.

### Demonstration

Show one educator moving from a real task to an editable Visible Thinking Plan in a few minutes. The demonstration should make the reasoning of the interaction visible: intended capability, hidden work, selected moments, feedback loop and changed-condition application.

### Early impact claim

The MVP helps educators design stronger evidence of learning without adding a surveillance layer or requiring agreement on a single institutional position about AI.

Do not claim proven learning gains, assessment validity, authorship verification or reduced workload until tested.

## 11. Persistence, memory and responsibility

### What may persist

- activity designs;
- educator-approved Visible Thinking Plans;
- reusable task-design patterns;
- educator revisions and preferences;
- lessons about which prompts or conditions improved the design interaction.

### What should not persist in the MVP

- identifiable learner records;
- learner performance profiles;
- raw classroom conversations;
- surveillance logs;
- inferred capability or risk scores attached to individuals.

### Responsibility created by persistence

If the system remembers an educator’s designs, it must preserve their authorship, allow correction and deletion, distinguish recommendations from approved practice, and avoid silently turning local preferences into universal pedagogy.

## 12. Design and safety principles

1. **Learning before performance.** Protect capability development beneath the output.
2. **Preserve consequential cognitive work.** Support participation without removing every meaningful decision, check or attempt.
3. **Guidance enables agency.** Scaffolding should improve inquiry rather than prescribe conclusions.
4. **Visibility without surveillance.** Select evidence that matters; do not capture everything.
5. **Product plus process.** The finished output remains evidence but does not interpret itself.
6. **Professional conversation strengthens evidence; it does not prove authorship.**
7. **Feedback must change what happens next.**
8. **Transfer requires changed conditions.**
9. **Confidence rests on converging evidence.**
10. **Human editorial authority remains explicit.** The educator reviews and edits the plan.
